title: "The Wind That Was Always There"
date: "2026-09-11"

A small village sat in a valley between two long hills.

The animals there were content. The badgers kept the storerooms. The marmots tended the gardens. The sparrows watched the sky. Each season followed the last, and the work was honest and slow.

There was a gap in the northern hills, and through that gap came the wind.

It was always there, and the animals thought nothing of it. A breeze through the valley was as ordinary as the daylight. Most days it was barely anything, just enough to lift the corner of a leaf and run through the tops of the grass. They liked it, when they noticed it at all. Wind was natural. Wind was simply part of the valley.

But some days the wind blew harder, and those were the days the animals minded.

On those days it banged the loose shutter on the meeting hall. It pushed at the old pine above the granary. It found the thin spot in the roof of the storeroom and whistled through it, low and steady, like something trying to be heard. The harder it blew, the more it bothered them, and the more they wished it would settle.

![](/images/blog/wind1.webp)

What none of them saw was that the bother was the point.

The strong wind rose before the rough weather. It leaned hardest on the weakest places, as if it were pointing straight at them. The very thing that annoyed the animals was the valley trying to tell them something.

But they could not hold the two thoughts at once. They knew the wind was natural, so when it blew hard they felt only a familiar wind turned rude. Each of them simply closed against it. The badger pulled his door without thinking. The marmot pressed her window shut and weighed the loose things down. The sparrows tucked into the eaves. No one spoke of it. No one decided to. It was just what you did when the air turned rough, the way you draw a cloak tighter without choosing to.

So no one asked why the wind rose, or what it leaned on, or whether it might be pointing at anything. Being annoyed was the whole of their understanding. It never occurred to them that to be bothered by the wind was to be warned by it.

They even built a fine new wall on the southern side of the village, where the wind never reached, because the southern side was the easiest place to build.

The gentle days they enjoyed. The hard days they shut out. And when the wind softened again, they opened their windows and thought no more about it.

One night the wind blew harder than it ever had. The shutter banged. The pine bent low. The thin roof moaned. The animals closed their doors and windows, weighed down the loose things as they always did, and went to sleep waiting for it to pass.

![](/images/blog/wind2.webp)

Behind the wind came a great storm, the kind the valley had no name for. It rolled over the northern hills in the dark and fell on the village all at once.

The loose shutter was the first to go.
Then the old pine, which came down across the granary just where it had always leaned.
Then the thin part of the roof above the storeroom, which opened like a seam and let the rain take the winter grain.

By morning the village was broken in exactly the places the wind had been touching for years.

The animals gathered in what was left of the meeting hall.

They had never seen anything like it.

"Unheard of," said the badger.
"A disaster out of nowhere," said the marmot.
"How could anyone have known?" asked an old sparrow.

They formed a council. They studied the wreckage and measured the fallen pine and the torn roof and the broken shutter, and they asked, over and over, how such a thing could arrive without a single warning.

No one thought of the wind. Why would they? They had spent their whole lives not feeling it. To them the storm had no past. It had simply come, the way a stranger comes, knocking once and leaving ruin.

And while they spoke, the breeze drifted through the open roof and stirred their papers. Without looking up, someone reached out and shut the window so the council could hear itself think.

In time, they rebuilt the village.

They hung a new shutter on the same hinge. They planted a young pine where the old one had leaned. They patched the thin roof with the same thin reeds, because that was how the roof had always been made.

All the while they worked, the wind moved gently through the valley, the way it always had, lifting the corner of a leaf, stirring the tops of the grass. None of them felt it.

![](/images/blog/wind3.webp)

When the village was whole again, the wind moved through the valley as it always had.

The grass bent. The flowers leaned. The shutter on the meeting hall stirred on its new hinge.

And as the wind rose, a little, and then a little more, until it began to bother them again, the animals reached out, without looking up, and shut their windows.

The grass bent harder.

Moral:
A warning does not always arrive looking like a warning. Sometimes it arrives as an annoyance, and once we have decided the wind is only ever the wind, we shut the window on the very thing that came to prepare us.
