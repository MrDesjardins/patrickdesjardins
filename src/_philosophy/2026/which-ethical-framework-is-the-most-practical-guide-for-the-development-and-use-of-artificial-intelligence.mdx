In this essay, I argue that deontology is the best ethical framework for guiding the design and behavior of artificial intelligence systems. The participation of artificial intelligence in decisions is increasing with the introduction of large language models. These models enable computers to reason from an enormous amount of past texts written by different people of different cultures, morals, beliefs, historical periods, and geographical regions. The diversity of this knowledge presents a challenge because users expect to receive a single best answer. Deontology provides a framework based on clearly defined moral laws that set aside self-interest and offer explicit instructions that computers can apply to a large range of questions (Kant, 1785/2012). By contrast, utilitarianism requires predicting future consequences and comparing different kinds of benefits and harms. While artificial intelligence can process enormous quantities of data, it struggles to accurately measure competing human values such as happiness. Similarly, virtue ethics is difficult to implement in artificial intelligence because virtues are not fixed rules but context-sensitive judgments. An artificial intelligence system would need to determine where the virtuous mean lies between two extremes in countless unique situations, a task that requires practical wisdom rather than the application of explicit principles.

My first thesis is that deontology provides explicit maxims that are universal and applied all the time. Computers operate efficiently with rules that are deterministic and universal as they can apply them through a structured decision process. In a scenario where a rule is missing, using a Kantian ethic, a new universal rule is added, the framework evolves and remains the same for future decisions. By acting according to duty, an artificial system focuses on the action and eliminates the complexity of calculating the ethical value of every consequence. Besides the convenience of performance to decide the best ethical response, the framework works well with many relevant maxims. The maxims can be consistently applied in a deterministic way without having a bias toward any particular consequences. Kant's a priori moral principles apply to humans throughout time and across cultures, matching the knowledge base of large language models that borrow empirical information from different sources (Kant, 1785/2012). Kant's Formula of the Kingdom of Ends states that people should act only on principles which could earn acceptance by a community (Kant, 1785/2012, pp. 82-83). The community can expand to the human species, which defines the maxims used to guide artificial intelligence systems.

My second thesis argues that utilitarianism is sub-optimal because artificial intelligence must include the consequences as part of the ethical evaluation. Like the trolley dilemma, evaluating what is ethically correct needs transforming qualitative situations into quantitative solutions. The process requires teaching the artificial intelligence what generates the greatest good or happiness across many situations. The teaching relies on a large amount of historical data which may include biases coming from location, time period, culture, and the composition of the training corpus. Conflicts may emerge in situations where many people are involved, leading to an impasse. Happiness is difficult to compare across individuals and circumstances, making utility calculations uncertain. According to Mill, pleasures are divided into higher and lower pleasures (Mill, 1863/2001, pp. 8-10), but the categorization is subjective and may not necessarily be universally applicable to an artificial system. Mill includes the reliance on primary principles when the secondary principles are in conflict. However, when primary principles conflict or the maximization of utility is subjective, artificial intelligence is in an impasse. As a result, utilitarianism determination of value by artificial intelligence is a complex and subjective task.
My third thesis concerns virtue ethics, which shares a strength of deontology by focusing on identifying virtues prior to a specific situation. However, virtue ethics has the additional challenge of evaluating virtues on scale. Aristotle mentions that a person's virtuousness varies according to a specific situation (Aristotle, ca. 350 BCE/2009). For example, too much bravery could lead to recklessness and cease to be virtuous; too little leads the same person toward cowardice. Determining the proper degree of virtue is unclear and difficult to codify without introducing personal preferences. The identification of a good character is complex compared to defining explicit rules. Aristotle mentions that it is a skill that requires experience and practice (Aristotle, ca. 350 BCE/2009). Any attempt to train an artificial intelligence to identify virtues would still depend on human judgments regarding which behaviors should be rewarded, introducing subjective preferences into the system. Consequently, virtue ethics is less clear for artificial intelligence compared to a rule-dependent framework.

Although deontology is the most appropriate framework for artificial intelligence, it is not without flaws. First, those who define the maxims are subject to bias, and determining what is categorical and imperative can be difficult. Hence, the establishment of a committee of different backgrounds and perspectives could help identify and reduce biases while keeping the universality of the maxims. Second, developing a corpus of maxims that cover a large set of human situations is an ongoing task. The challenge can be improved using such a committee that works toward expanding and refining the maxims while keeping them as general as possible. Third, conflicts among the maxims may happen. To avoid debating when determining if a situation is ethical or not, artificial intelligence might require a hierarchy where the maxims are organized by precedence. These flaws are manageable before determining the morality of a specific situation, which allows artificial intelligence systems apply ethical principles without relying on predictions of future outcomes.

In conclusion, deontology is the most practical guide for artificial intelligence because it shifts ethical complexity to human reasoning through the establishment of universal maxims. Unlike utilitarianism, we clarified that artificial intelligence analysis is simplified because it does not require calculation of consequences for every situation. Similarly, the deontology framework is less subjective than virtue ethics, which requires identifying the proper virtue and mean with the proper sensitivity of virtuousness in every situation. While deontology requires human effort to create the maxims, it remains under supervision and control of humans, making it explicit to other humans who want to understand precisely which maxim impacted the ethical decision. The ease of understanding the transgression of a maxim by a human is transferred to the artificial intelligence systems that use the same set of maxims for their ethical responses. In short, humans can inspect, debate, revise, and challenge maxims while artificial intelligence applies them consistently across situations.
